Reasons for Toxic Air

5. URBAN HEAT ISLAND (UHI) - A self-perpetuating problem

The Urban Heat Island (UHI), is a phenomenon where near-surface air temperatures are higher than their non-urban surroundings, particularly at night.

Built urban sites have large areas of exposed surface per unit area of ground cover. Therefore, potentially more solar radiation can be collected on a built urban site than on flat open terrain. In urban areas, the lack of vegetation converts more incoming energy into sensible heat whereas, in rural areas, the energy is transformed into latent heat. The sensible heat is stored in the urban fabric and released slowly causing an urban heat island in the nighttime.

The primary factors contributing to this phenomenon are the properties of urban infrastructures, reduced sky visibility, decreased relative humidity and latent heat flux, increased sensible heat flux, diminished vegetation coverage, and limited open space in urban environments.


Rising pollution levels in urban areas may amplify the UHI effect because of the radiative impacts of various atmospheric pollutants that harm human health and contribute to smog formation and sometimes acid rain.


Dense urban areas with concrete structures and limited greenery absorb and retain heat, raising local temperatures and altering air circulation. This traps pollutants like PM, NOx, and ozone, while promoting secondary pollutant formation (e.g., ozone via VOC-NOx reactions).
