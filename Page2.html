2. BUILDING DOWNWASH - A Civil Engineering

Building Downwash is a wind-related phenomenon where wind striking tall buildings and/or structures, is deflected downwards, creating strong downdrafts at street level. The intensity of downwash is influenced by factors like building height, shape, orientation, and the surrounding urban environment.

Buildings and similar structures in the path of air flow create a turbulent wake region, which is a low pressure area on the leeward (i.e., downwind) side of the building. A plume caught in the path of this flow is drawn into this area called the wake, temporarily trapping it in a recirculating cavity.

This downwash effect leads to higher ground-level pollutant concentrations near the building than if the building was not present.

Downwash effects can be classified as:

  1. Stack downwash, which is a possible drag of the effluent in the wake downwind of an emitting stack due to the presence of the stack itself.
  2. Building downwash, which can occur when an effluent is emitted near a building and is brought downward by the flow of air over and around the building.

Stack and building downwash cause an increase of ground-level concentrations, because of two concomitant phenomena:

  • (i) The drag of the effluent in the stack and/or building wake—since this wake extends below the stack outlet, this drag causes the plume to decrease in height;
  • (ii) The increase of entrainment with ambient air due to wake turbulence—causing a consequent decrease of buoyancy in case of buoyant emissions.

Building downwash effects are the cause of maximum concentrations in approximately 90% of dispersion modelling studies.
