Reasons for Toxic Air

4. THE CANOPY EFFECT - When Trees Increase Pollution

The canopy effect in urban areas refers to how trees and other vegetation influence air pollution levels by modifying wind patterns, temperature, and pollutant concentrations.

While vegetation can help remove pollutants like PM2.5 and ozone through processes like deposition (particles sticking to leaves) and stomatal uptake (gases entering leaves through small pores), it can also trap pollutants within street canyons, because of the geometric structure of urban canyons, with buildings and by hindering wind flow. Trees can also emit volatile organic compounds (VOCs), which can react with other pollutants like nitrogen oxides (NOx) to form ozone.

Tree crowns also generate airflow resistance, which reduces turbulent kinetic energy, and change wind protiles, and cause local reversal, thereby promoting particle aggregation or re-suspension. Evidence indicates that higher pollutant concentrations are more likely to occur under denser tree canopies.

Overall, the canopy effect is complex and depends on factors like canopy density, tree species, urban geometry, and meteorological conditions. While urban greening can contribute to air quality improvement, it's not a simple fix and can sometimes exacerbate pollution in certain areas.
